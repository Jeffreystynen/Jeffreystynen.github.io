Fat Chance: Probability from the Ground Up

Harvard edx
Course Overview

During my final year, I took the online course Fat Chance: Probability from the Ground Up, offered by Harvard University through edX. The course was designed to teach the basics of probability in a clear and practical way. It used real-life examples to explain how chance works and how we can use it to make better decisions.

Topics Covered

The course began with basic counting methods, which are important for understanding how probability works. As the course continued, these ideas were expanded and used to solve more complex problems. Key topics included:

  • Basic and advanced counting techniques
  • Introduction to probability and how to calculate simple probabilities
  • Conditional probability and how one event can affect another
  • Expected value and how to understand the average result of many trials
  • Bernoulli trials – repeated experiments with two possible outcomes
  • The normal distribution and how it is used to estimate probabilities in real life
  • Using probability to understand and work with uncertainty
The course used visual lessons and real-life examples to explain each concept in a way that was clear and easy to follow.

What I Learned

By the end of the course, I had gained a more refined understanding of probability and statistics and its applications. This knowledge has changed my perspective on how I approach problems and make decisions, with a more mathamatical mindset. This course, so far, has helped me during several projects, such as the ANPR monitoring system and my internship at Mobilab&Care.

Machine Learning & Causal Inference: A Short Course

Stanford Graduate School of Buisiness
Course Overview

During my internship at Mobilab&Care, I enrolled in the online course Machine Learning & Causal Inference: A Short Course offered by Stanford Graduate School of Business. This course introduced the core concepts of machine learning and how they can be applied to understand cause-and-effect relationships in data. It provided me with a strong foundation in both areas, which I was able to apply during my internship projects.

Topics Covered

This course focused on how machine learning can be used to understand cause-and-effect relationships in real-world data. It combined ideas from economics, statistics, and computer science. Key topics included:

  • Applied Machine Learning: Supervised learning techniques with a focus on how economists use them in practice.
  • Prediction vs. Estimation: Understanding the difference between using models to predict outcomes and using them to estimate causal effects.
  • Average Treatment Effects (ATE): Methods to estimate the effect of a treatment or action on an entire population, including propensity scores and double robustness.
  • Conditional Average Treatment Effects (CATE): Using causal trees and causal forests to estimate how treatment effects vary across individuals or groups.
  • Robust Estimation: Designing machine learning algorithms that give reliable causal results, even when working with imperfect data.
  • Loss Functions for Causal Inference: Best practices for building and evaluating models that estimate treatment effects, with a focus on policy learning and validation.
The course used case studies and examples from economics and public policy to explain each concept in a clear and practical way.

What I Learned

This was a challenging course for me, as it required a background in statistics and econometrics. While I was not able to follow every concept in full detail, I gained a solid overview of the main ideas in causal inference.

I learned the difference between correlation and causation, and how confounding can affect the results of a study. I also understood what Average Treatment Effects (ATE) are and how Conditional Average Treatment Effects (CATE) can be estimated using advanced techniques like double robust methods. These concepts were new to me, but very eye-opening.

My earlier course, Fat Chance: Probability from the Ground Up, helped me understand the statistical thinking behind many of the lessons. Thanks to this course, I was also able to better understand the data analysis and evaluation work I did during my internship at Mobilab&Care.

ANPR Monitoring

Project Goal

The goal of this project was to enhance the monitoring and diagnostic capabilities of ANPR cameras by introducing a priority-based alert classification system. This helps reduce alert fatigue for system administrators and ensures faster response to critical issues.

Project Realisation

We developed a .NET application that provides an overview of all ANPR cameras and their error statuses. To support this, we built the necessary infrastructure, including a backend on AWS and secure communication via Tailscale VPN. We implemented a system to classify error messages by priority and monitored electrical components such as power supplies and fuses. I partly contributed to the Raspberry Pi integration used for monitoring electrical components. My main focus was on designing the data handling and alert prioritization system. This mathematical approach allowed us to evaluate error conditions using weighted scores. It resulted in a system that is not only faster but also more cost-efficient to run.

Self driving car

Thomas More
Project Goal

In this project, we built a self-driving car model for Forza Horizon 4. Our aim was to create an autonomous system that can drive the car on virtual roads using deep learning. We also developed a separate object detection model with YOLO to spot other vehicles and obstacles. Although running both models at the same time wasn't possible because of hardware limits, each part helped us learn more about different aspects of autonomous driving.

Project Realisation

We started by collecting data with a custom script that captured screenshots of the game every 0.5 seconds. To focus on the road, we cropped out the top 30% of each image. We then enhanced the images by rotating, zooming, and adjusting brightness. One challenge was that most images showed straight roads, so we duplicated images with sharp turns to help the model learn how to handle them. After cleaning and preparing the data, we trained our model using a ResNet architecture. Through several training rounds and adjustments, the model learned to drive the car smoothly in different conditions.

My Contribution

I was responsible for gathering and cleaning the game data, ensuring that each image was paired with the correct controller inputs. I built a program that converts the model’s output into real controller actions, such as steering angles and throttle values. By repeating images of sharp turns, I helped balance our dataset so the model could learn to handle curves better. This hands-on work not only improved my deep learning skills but also taught me valuable lessons in teamwork and problem solving.

Agentic AI

Thomas More
Project Goal

In this project, we built a personalized learning assistant that can take PDF documents and answer questions about their content. Using CrewAI, Streamlit, and Groq’s language model, our goal was to create a tool that shows how to turn uploaded PDFs into an interactive Q&A experience. Inspired by Akansha Saxena, we aimed to tackle both the technical and practical challenges of connecting document processing with dynamic question answering.

Project Realisation

We built the project by first using Streamlit to create a simple chat interface and file uploader. Users can easily upload PDF files, which are then processed by our assistant. The system uses CrewAI to manage two agents: one to ingest and store content from the PDFs into a vector database (using ChromaDB) and the other to answer questions based on that stored data. Communication between these components is handled via a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipeline that integrates Groq’s language model for generating context-aware responses. We also developed tools like ProcessFile and ProcessInput to ensure smooth handling of file uploads and chat interactions.

My Contribution

In this project, my main contribution was developing the tools that our agents rely on to complete their tasks. I created the ProcessFile tool, which manages PDF uploads by temporarily storing each file, reading its contents, and feeding the data into our vector database. I also built the ProcessInput tool, which captures user queries from the Streamlit chat interface, passes them to our system, and retrieves relevant information using our Retrieval-Augmented Generation pipeline. These tools streamlined content ingestion and question answering, allowing our agents to work together smoothly and efficiently.

Calculus

Self taught
Study Overview

Out of personal interest, I began working through the Calculus textbook by Adams (9th edition, Pearson). Although this was not part of a formal course, I chose to study it independently in my spare time because a strong foundation in mathematics is essential in computer science.

So far, I have completed roughly one-third of the book. This self-study is helping me gain a deeper understanding of key mathematical ideas and has already improved the way I approach problems in technical subjects.

Topics Covered

Below is a list of chapters and topics I have studied so far from the textbook:

  • Chapter 0 – Preliminaries: Real numbers, inequalities, graphs, functions, trigonometric functions
  • Chapter 1 – Limits: Introduction to limits, rigorous definitions, limit theorems, trigonometric limits, infinite limits, continuity
  • Chapter 2 – The Derivative: Definition and interpretation of the derivative, rules of differentiation, chain rule, higher-order derivatives, implicit differentiation, related rates, differentials
  • Chapter 3 – Applications of the Derivative: Maxima and minima, concavity, graphing, the Mean Value Theorem, numerical methods, Taylor polynomials, Big-O notation, antiderivatives, intro to differential equations
  • Chapter 4 – The Definite Integral: Area, definition of the integral, Fundamental Theorems of Calculus, substitution, numerical methods
  • Chapter 5 – Applications of the Integral: Area under curves, volumes, arc length, work, center of mass, and probability
  • Chapter 6 – Transcendental Functions: Exponential and logarithmic functions, growth/decay, inverse trig functions, differential equations, further use of Taylor polynomials
  • Chapter 7 – Techniques of Integration: Basic rules, integration by parts, trigonometric integrals, partial fractions, and strategy
What I Learned

Working through this material has increased my mathematical maturity, especially when it comes to formal reasoning and abstract thinking. I found some sections challenging, but the effort has paid off by strengthening my grasp of important concepts such as limits, derivatives, and integrals. These ideas are foundational in areas like optimization, differential equations, and even aspects of machine learning.

This study has also helped me better understand topics in other courses, particularly the Stanford course on causal inference, which required a more statistical background. Thanks to the calculus work I’ve done here, I was better prepared to engage with ideas like expected value, rates of change, and function behavior.
